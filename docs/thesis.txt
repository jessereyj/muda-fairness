Abstract

This thesis presents a formal temporal verification of fairness properties in the Multi-
Unit Double Auction (MUDA) protocol. We verify six fairness properties for all
infinite execution traces generated by the deterministic STS, formalized in Linear
Temporal Logic (LTL). The MUDA protocol describes buyer bid submission, seller ask
submission, multi-unit matching at a single uniform clearing price, and trade execution
across seven protocol phases. Each execution state records bids, asks, quantities,
matches, and clearing price. These records are used as atomic predicates for the
fairness properties, namely, Priority Fairness, Quantity Fairness, Price Fairness, Match
Finality, Maximality, and Justified Rejection. This thesis formalizes MUDA as a
deterministic STS and expresses fairness properties using the temporal operators G
(always), F (eventually), and U (until). We mechanize the MUDA-specific operational
semantics and fairness theorems in the Rocq proof assistant (formerly known as Coq).
All fairness proofs are provided without admitted lemmas, while selected algorithmic
properties and standard logical results are used as axioms from the literature. For
LTL reasoning, we adopt standard soundness and adequacy results from the literature
rather than re-mechanizing completeness. Eight simulation scenarios illustrate these
properties on concrete executions.
     Keywords: Auction fairness, Linear Temporal Logic, Matching algorithms,
Mechanized proofs, Multi-Unit Double Auction, Rocq proof assistant, STSs, Uniform
Pricing.

Chapter 1

Introduction

    A MUDA is a mechanism in which multiple sellers submit asks and multiple

buyers submit bids to trade multiple units of a homogeneous good at a single uniform

clearing price [10], [11], [12], [20]. During order submission, multiple sellers specify

the quantities of goods they offer and their minimum acceptable prices, while multiple

buyers specify the quantities of goods they require and their maximum acceptable

prices. As illustrated in Figure A.1, a trade becomes feasible when a buyer’s bid price

meets or exceeds a seller’s ask price. Then, the MUDA protocol determines which

buyers and sellers participate in trades, how many units are transferred, and the price

per trade [10], [11].

    MUDA mechanisms are used in stock markets, treasury auctions, energy markets,

and cloud resource allocation systems [1], [2], [10], [11], [12], [20].

    As illustrated in Figure A.2, MUDA is positioned among other typical auction

models. A single-unit auction engages multiple buyers purchasing from a single seller.

A double auction engages multiple buyers and multiple sellers purchasing a single unit.


                                             1
In a continuous double auction, orders are submitted and matched over a period as the

market changes. However, for MUDA, the process takes place in a batch execution,

whereby multiple buyers and multiple sellers place their bids and asks simultaneously.

Each of these agents can sell and buy multiple units at a single unit price.

    For this reason, MUDA operates as a finite sequence of state transitions. Matching,

quantity updates, pricing, and rejection occur within one execution. The finite state

sequence is extended to an infinite execution trace by stuttering at the terminal state.

This extension permits temporal reasoning over complete executions.

    Existing studies establish economic properties of MUDA under uniform pricing

[10], [11], [12]. These include IR, IC, BB, and AE. The analysis in this literature

focuses on truthful bidding and allocation results under equilibrium assumptions.

However, the literature does not present a state-based execution model for MUDA.

Specifically, it does not specify how priority is assigned when several feasible trades exist

simultaneously, nor how residual quantities are processed during trades. Moreover, it

does not provide a state-based condition explaining why some orders trade and others

do not. Prior formal verification efforts focus on single-unit auctions or continuous

double auctions and are not applicable to batch auctioning with a single overall clearing

price in MUDA [17], [18], [19].

    Classical logics such as first-order logic and Hoare logic evaluate formulae at a

single state or over a single program state [22], [23], [28], [29]. As a result, they do

not express temporal properties that must hold across an entire execution trace. LTL

evaluates formulae along positions of a fixed trace and provides temporal operators

that refer to future states on that trace [25], [26], [30]. This expressiveness is required



                                             2
to state persistence and eventuality conditions in auction executions. For example,

priority fairness requires that the priority condition holds at every state of the execution

trace. Therefore, temporal reasoning is necessary for formal verification of all six

fairness properties.

    This thesis fills these gaps by achieving the following objectives. First, it formal-

izes the operational semantics of MUDA as a deterministic STS with seven phases:

submission, sorting, matching, clearing price, finalization, bookkeeping, and rejection.

These phases are partitioned into pre-match, settlement, and post-match stages, as

shown in Figure A.3. Second, it defines the six fairness properties in LTL using the

temporal operators G (always), F (eventually), and U (until). These properties are

Priority Fairness, Quantity Fairness, Price Fairness, Match Finality, Maximality, and

Justified Rejection. Third, it formally verifies the fairness properties using the Rocq

[27] proof assistant. All fairness proofs are provided without admitted lemmas. This

verification proves that these properties can hold for all infinite traces generated by

the deterministic transition function.

    This thesis investigates three research questions:

   1. How can MUDA execution be formalized as a deterministic STS?

   2. Which temporal operators are sufficient to express fairness properties over MUDA

      execution traces?

   3. Can all six fairness properties be verified using Rocq?

    In this thesis, the MUDA protocol is formalized as a deterministic STS and

single-round auction, disregarding strategic bids. The protocol is batch-oriented,


                                             3
analyzing all bids and asks at once. The MUDA protocol is assumed to operate under

truthful bids, where buyers and sellers submit orders according to their valuation.

This matches the MUDA variants studied in the economic literature [10], [11], [20] and

limits the scope of the verification to fairness properties over execution traces. The

other auction properties related to strategic manipulation, collusion, or round-robin

auction systems are not considered in this research. Future verification work may

consider extending the results into nondeterministic models of auction systems.

    This thesis presents temporal verification of MUDA fairness properties over

complete executions in a deterministic STS, with all proofs machine checked in the

Rocq proof assistant [27].

Chapter 2

Literature Review


2.1     Related Work

    Literature on MUDA has been conducted primarily on the economic properties in

stock markets, treasury auctions, energy trading markets, and cloud resource allocation

systems [10], [11], [12], [20]. Other literature has been conducted on fairness-related

notions, but these are not defined in terms of temporal properties in the execution of

a protocol. The following discussion is on how existing results are related to MUDA

on different properties.


2.1.1     Auction Economic Properties

    Existing literature has been conducted on the economic properties of MUDA, such

as IR, IC, BB, and AE [10], [11], [12], [20]. The literature has been conducted on how

truthful bidding is conducted in the market, where buyers place bids and sellers place

asks under truthful bidding conditions. Other literature has been conducted on welfare,

                                          5
equilibrium, and incentive in different types of auction systems, such as double-sided

auctions, treasury auctions, combinatorial markets, and repeated single-sided auctions

[2], [9], [14]. The double-sided auction mechanism by McAfee is cited in this case, but

it is limited to only single-unit trade.


2.1.2     Fairness Definitions in Auction Mechanisms

    Each study has defined its own concept of fairness in auction mechanisms and has

used a set of criteria that is related to the objective of the specific mechanism used in

the auction.

    A study by Bartolini and Cottarelli presents the concept of the fairness of the

auction held by the treasury with respect to the symmetry of information on the

orders [2]. In this study, the concept of fairness was related to the symmetry of the

information held by the groups that participated in the auction. In this study, the

auction was single-sided, meaning that only one group of agents participated in the

auction by placing the orders. Therefore, this concept of fairness is not related to the

allocation of the items between the groups in the MUDA.

    Murillo et al. proposed the concept of the fairness of the auction held in the

next rounds of the auction with respect to the symmetry of the distributions of the

items [16]. Therefore, the concept of the fairness of the auction was related to the

distributions of the items. However, the auction held in the MUDA was double-sided

and was held in one round. Therefore, the proposed concept of the fairness of the

auction is not related to the proposed auction mechanism.

    The concept of the fairness of the auction proposed by Liu et al. was related



                                           6
to the concept of the two-sided auction with budgets [14]. In this concept of the

fairness of the auction, the number of participants was related to the budgets of the

participants. However, this concept of the fairness of the auction was not related to

the priority constraints, the constant price level, or the matching order constraints.

Therefore, this concept of the fairness of the auction was not related to the fairness of

the auction held in the MUDA.


2.1.3      Auction Clearing Price Rules

     The literature on the auction mechanism presents the use of two different clearing

price rules for the MUDA, namely discriminatory pricing or uniform pricing [2], [8],

[10], [11], [15].

     In the analysis of the use of uniform pricing, Markakis and Telelis identify the

stability conditions in which the trader cannot influence the prices [15]. In the same

context, the use of the system of uniform prices, MUDA, was identified by Huang et

al. as the basis for the calculation of the assignment of prices using the negotiation

scheme [10]. Although the focus was on the use of the negotiation scheme with the

use of the clearing price, none of the above sources considered the issue of uniqueness

of the value.

     During MUDA execution, the set clearing price rule should always be within the

range of the marginal bid and offer for feasibility. These are derived from the process

of matching and should always be satisfied during execution.




                                           7
2.1.4     Formal Verification of Trades and Auctions

    Interactive theorem proving techniques have been applied in auctions for formal

verification. However, current formal verification is focused on correctness properties

for specific auction models. No execution behavior is taken into consideration in

current formal verification techniques. In their work, Natarajan et al. use a Coq proof

assistant for formal verification of double-sided call auctions for multi-unit orders.

They have shown formal verification for optimal uniform match and maximum volume

of trade using a Coq proof assistant [17]. In their earlier work, Sarswat and Singh have

shown formal verification for fairness, uniformity, and individual rationality in single

unit auctions. Match predicates have been formalized and correctness properties have

been shown for specific final results of auctions [18], [19].

    In their recent work on formal verification for auctions, Garg and Sarswat have

specified continuous double auction rules in Coq proof assistants for showing correctness

properties for order matching in auctions with price-time priority and partial fills for

sequential order arrival [6].

    In addition, Garg et al. design an automated checker for double auctions and

prove uniqueness properties that facilitate the verification of violations by comparing

the output from the exchange and the verified specifications [7]. Following this, the

automated checker is used to verify the output from the final auctions, helping to

identify inconsistencies in the output and the verified algorithms.

    Among all the contributions, the execution of the auction is defined in the

algorithms. The bids and asks are ranked according to the level of competitiveness,

and the feasibility is measured by considering the comparisons between the bid and


                                            8
ask prices, and the trade sizes are computed according to the remaining sizes. The

matching is performed until the termination conditions are satisfied. However, there is

no formalization of MUDA as a deterministic batch multi-unit protocol that is suitable

for temporal verification of the execution phase’s fairness properties. Moreover, there

is no formalization or verification of the fairness properties as temporal relationships

between the execution traces. Thus, the six fairness properties of MUDA are not

addressed in the verification studies conducted so far.

Chapter 3

Methodology

    This chapter presents the operational semantics of the MUDA protocol. The

researcher models the MUDA protocol as a deterministic STS [26], [29]. This follows

the description of the MUDA protocol as outlined in the last chapter of the thesis [10],

[20]. MUDA is structurally different from single unit auctions, as any agent can trade

more than one unit, which requires the tracking of residual quantities after any trade.

MUDA is also structurally different from continuous double auctions, as in CDA,

matching occurs incrementally, with a single uniform price being determined only after

matching has stopped. Moreover, MUDA includes priority rules to determine which

of the possible buyer–seller pairs will be selected in cases where there are multiple

possible trades simultaneously. These differences require the state-based model of

transitions, which includes residual tracking, priority ordering, and a well-defined

termination state. All definitions, assumptions, propositions, and notation used later

will be introduced in this chapter.

3.1        Object and State Representation

    This section provides the representation of each object and state for the specifica-

tion of the MUDA protocol. Both buyers and sellers are active agents in bidding or

asking price during the MUDA execution trace.

    Let B = {b1 , . . . , bm } be a finite set of buyers, where m ≥ 1. Let S = {s1 , . . . , sn }

be a finite set of sellers, where n ≥ 1. Let


                             x = (B, S, O, resid, M, p? , phase)


be a state, where O denotes the submitted orders of the buyers and sellers, resid

denotes the residual quantity function on agents, M denotes the match record of

trades between buyers and sellers, p? denotes the clearing price p? determined after

matching terminates, and phase(x) ∈ {P1 , . . . , P7 }.

    Here, resid is a function that assigns to each agent its residual unit quantity in

state x.

    Each buyer makes exactly one order to the market. The order made by each

buyer is represented as bi = (pi , qi0 , ti ), where pi is the buyer’s bid unit price, qi0 is the

buyer’s initial unit quantity, and ti is the buyer’s submission time. The buyer selector

functions p(bi ), q(bi ), and t(bi ) extract the bid unit price, initial unit quantity, and

submission time, respectively.

    In the same way, each seller makes exactly one order to the market. The order

made by each seller is represented as sj = (aj , qj0 , tj ), where aj is the seller’s ask unit

price, qj0 is the seller’s initial unit quantity, and tj is the seller’s submission time. The



                                               11
seller selector functions a(sj ), q(sj ), and t(sj ) extract the ask unit price, initial unit

quantity, and submission time, respectively.

    Once each order has been submitted by any agent, the components of the or-

der remain constant throughout the execution trace of the MUDA protocol. This

means that the deterministic state-transition function does not change the unit price,

submission time, or initial unit quantity of any agent.

    For each buyer bi ∈ B, the buyer residual quantity residB(bi , x) ∈ N represents

the portion of the buyer’s initial unit quantity that has not yet been matched. In

the same way, for each seller sj ∈ S, the seller residual quantity residS(sj , x) ∈ N

represents the portion of the seller’s initial unit quantity that has also not yet been

matched.

    The match record M (x) is a finite sequence of trades between buyers and sellers

that have been executed up to state x. The match record M (x) represents all the

history of matching decisions generated during the execution trace of the MUDA

protocol. Each item in M (x) is a triple of the form (bi , sj , q), where bi is a buyer in B,

sj is a seller in S, and q is a positive unit quantity.

    The structure of the finite sequence follows the order in which trades are executed

during Phase P3 . Each item in M (x) represents one application of the matching rule.

Once an item is put into M (x), it remains constant throughout the execution trace of

the MUDA protocol.

    Let xhalt be the first state such that phase(xhalt ) = P4 . Let M fin = M (xhalt )

denote the final match record at the end of Phase P3 , and let K = |M fin |.

3.2       Execution Trace and Deterministic State-

          Transition Function

    The execution trace of the MUDA protocol is given by a deterministic state-

transition function [26], [29].

    Let δ be a function from State to State [26]. Then, the state δ(x) for each state x

is uniquely determined by the state transition function associated with phase(x).

    Let x0 be an initial state. Then, the execution trace derived from x0 is represented

by a function σ such that σ(0) = x0 and σ(i + 1) = δ(σ(i)) for all integers i ≥ 0 [26],

[29].

    Since the function δ is deterministic, the trace σ is uniquely determined by the

initial state x0 .

    The termination of the MUDA protocol is achieved when phase(x) = P7 . For

each state x such that phase(x) = P7 , it holds that δ(x) = x. Then, the execution

trace proceeds by repeating the same state.



3.3       Structural Assumptions

    The following assumptions describe structural properties of the deterministic STS

that models MUDA execution trace.

Assumption 1 (Order components remain constant). Order components remain

constant throughout the execution trace of the MUDA protocol. For each buyer bi ∈ B,

the values p(bi ), q(bi ), and t(bi ) are the same in x and in δ(x). In the same way, for



                                           13
each seller sj ∈ S, the values a(sj ), q(sj ), and t(sj ) are also the same in x and in

δ(x).

Assumption 2 (Phase determinism). The state transition function assigns exactly

one successor to each state. For each state x, there exists one and only one state δ(x)

[26]. Hence, no alternative successor state is possible from the same state x. Thus, the

next state is determined by the current state, and the execution trace does not branch

at any state.

Assumption 3 (Terminal phase is absorbing). For each state x, if phase(x) = P7 ,

then δ(x) = x. In this situation, the transition function leaves the state unchanged.

Hence, the terminal phase always preserves the state.

3.5         MUDA Protocol Phases

    The MUDA protocol has seven phases in its execution. The phases of the protocol

are denoted by P1 to P7 . The protocol follows the batch protocol execution in [10],

[20]. The phase of the protocol, denoted by phase(x), determines which transition

rule is executed by the deterministic function δ.


3.5.1       Pre-Match Stage

    The pre-match stage is the stage of the protocol that prepares the MUDA state

prior to any trade. During this stage, no trade is executed.


Phase P1 (Submission)

    In Phase P1 of the protocol, B and S are fixed. The components of orders in B

and S are fixed. The residual unit quantity of buyer b ∈ B satisfies residB(b, x) = q(b).

The residual unit quantity of seller s ∈ S satisfies residS(s, x) = q(s). The match

record of x satisfies M (x) = ∅. The transition of the protocol to Phase P2 is made by

δ(x). The new protocol state satisfies phase(δ(x)) = P2 . The deterministic function δ

preserves B, S, O, resid, and M .




                                           18
Phase P2 (Sorting)

    In Phase P2 of the protocol, buyers in B and sellers in S are sorted according to

their priority relations defined in Definition 3. The residual unit quantities of buyers

in B and sellers in S do not change. The match record of x does not change. The

transition of the protocol to Phase P3 is made by δ(x). The new protocol state satisfies

phase(δ(x)) = P3 . Then, the deterministic function δ preserves B, S, O, resid, and

M . No trade is executed in this phase.


3.5.2     Matching Stage

    The matching stage is the stage of the protocol in which trades are executed

according to the feasibility condition and the priority relations.


Phase P3 (Matching)

    In Phase P3 of the protocol, the state x always satisfies phase(x) = P3 . If there

is a buyer b ∈ B and a seller s ∈ S such that b and s are feasible in state x, then a

trade is executed according to the priority rule defined in Definition 3 and the greedy

matching rule defined in Definition 6. The protocol stays in Phase P3 for as long as

there is a feasible buyer–seller pair.

    If there is no feasible buyer–seller pair in state x, then no trade is executed. In

this case, the transition of the protocol to Phase P4 is made by δ(x). The new state

of the protocol always satisfies phase(δ(x)) = P4 . Then, the deterministic function δ

preserves B, S, O, resid, and M .




                                          19
3.5.3     Settlement Stage

    The settlement stage determines the clearing price after the matching process has

ended.


Phase P4 (Clearing Price)

    In Phase P4 of the protocol, the clearing price p? is given by Definition 9. No

trade takes place in this phase. The sets B and S, the orders O, the residual function

resid, and the match record M remain unchanged. The transition to Phase P5 of

the protocol is achieved by the function δ(x). The new state of the protocol satisfies

phase(δ(x)) = P5 .


3.5.4     Post-Match Stage

    The post-match stage is the final stage in the completion of the protocol.


Phase P5 (Finalization)

    In Phase P5 of the protocol, the final match record M fin is given by M (x). Except

for the phase component, no other component of the state is changed. The transition

to Phase P6 of the protocol is achieved by the function δ(x). The new state of the

protocol satisfies phase(δ(x)) = P6 .


Phase P6 (Bookkeeping)

    In Phase P6 of the protocol, derived values can be computed using M fin . The

sets B and S, the orders O, the residual function resid, the match record M , and


                                          20
the clearing price p? remain unchanged. The transition to Phase P7 of the protocol is

achieved by the function δ(x). The new state of the protocol satisfies phase(δ(x)) = P7 .


Phase P7 (Rejection)

    In Phase P7 of the protocol, the rejection of agents is given by Definition 10. All

components of the state remain unchanged. For every state x, if phase(x) = P7 , then

δ(x) = x. Thus, Phase P7 is the terminal phase of the MUDA protocol. The trace of

the protocol continues by repeating the same state.



3.6     Operational Properties

    The following propositions describe structural properties of the execution in each

phase of the MUDA protocol. These properties follow directly from the definitions

and the phase rules stated earlier.


Proposition 1 (Residual Non-negativity). For every buyer b ∈ B and every

seller s ∈ S, we have that residB(b, x) ≥ 0 and residS(s, x) ≥ 0 for every state x [20].

    The residual unit quantities are initialized to nonnegative values in Phase P1 ,

namely the initial unit quantities. During the matching phase, a residual unit quantity

is decreased by no more than the traded unit quantity q, which is defined as the

minimum of two nonnegative residual unit quantities (Definition 2).




                                           21
Proposition 2 (Conservation of Quantity in Phase P3 ). For all states x such

that phase(x) = P3 , and all buyers b ∈ B and all sellers s ∈ S,


 residB(b, x) = q(b) − allocB(b, M (x)),         residS(s, x) = q(s) − allocS(s, M (x)).


    In Phase P3 , each executed trade decreases the residual unit quantity of each

participating agent by exactly the traded unit quantity recorded in the appended

match triple. The matching rule does not introduce new unit quantities and does

not subtract unit quantities except those recorded in the match record. Hence, the

residual unit quantity of each agent equals its initial unit quantity minus the total

unit quantity traded by that agent.


Proposition 3 (Halting Condition of Phase P3 ). Given a state x such that

phase(x) = P3 , Phase P3 halts at x if and only if there do not exist b ∈ B and s ∈ S

such that b and s are feasible in x [20].

    Feasibility is defined in Definition 1. The matching phase continues as long as there

exists at least one feasible buyer–seller pair. Otherwise, no trade can be performed,

and the matching phase terminates.


Proposition 4 (Transition from P3 to P4 ). If phase(x) = P3 and there is no

feasible buyer–seller pair in x, then phase(δ(x)) = P4 , and δ preserves B, S, O, resid,

and M .

    In this case, the matching process terminates. Since no trade takes place, the

protocol transitions to the settlement stage without changing the other components of

the state.

Proposition 5 (Clearing Price Stability After Matching). Let x be a state

this phase, the clearing price component is not modified by subsequent protocol phases.

Thus, it is stable across P4 –P7 .

Chapter 4

Formal Logic, Proofs, and

Verification

    This chapter defines the formal language, trace semantics, and proof system used

to verify fairness properties of MUDA executions in Linear Temporal Logic (LTL).

We view a MUDA execution as a trace by evaluating each designated operational

predicate at each state and recording its truth value as an atomic proposition. We fix a

mapping from designated operational predicates to indices ι ∈ PROP; at a state x, the

valuation [[x ]] assigns each such index the truth value of its corresponding predicate

evaluated in x. The term “atomic proposition” is used in this sense throughout the

chapter [25], [26].

    The interpretation of LTL formulae over infinite traces uses the temporal operators

X, U , F , and G [25], [26]. A Hilbert-style proof system is used, and the standard

notions of validity and satisfiability are used as the meta-theoretic concepts [22], [23],

[24]. The soundness, completeness, and consistency of the adopted Hilbert-style system


                                           24
are considered standard results in the literature, and the proofs are not repeated in

the thesis [25], [26]. These results are used as background information to relate the

semantic notions of validity to derivability. The MUDA-specific results are proved

using the operational semantics and are mechanically verified in Rocq [20], [27].

    Because the transition function δ is deterministic, each initial state induces a

unique execution. By stuttering after termination, we obtain an infinite trace for

interpretation in the temporal semantics developed in this chapter [25], [26].

    The framework has three layers.

  1. Foundation layer. LTL syntax, trace semantics, axioms, and adopted meta-

     theorems [22], [23], [24], [25], [26].

  2. MUDA protocol layer. MUDA executions mapped to infinite traces by

     stuttering after termination [20], [25], [26].

  3. Fairness verification layer. Fairness formulae proved for all traces by invari-

     ants and termination arguments [20], [25], [26].

    All MUDA-specific lemmas and theorems have corresponding proofs that are

mechanically verified in the Rocq development described in Appendix B. The proofs

presented in this chapter summarize the structure of those mechanically verified

arguments [27].

4.1.1         Syntax

    Let PROP := N be the index set of atomic propositions [25], [26]. Each atomic

proposition is given an index ι, where the index set is given by PROP [25], [26]. The

index set PROP is used for bookkeeping purposes. It is used to refer to atoms while

maintaining the invariant that the state-level predicates are mapped to logical atoms

in a fixed way.

    The language L is defined to be the set of formulae constructed from the set of

atomic propositions using a small number of logical and temporal operators. It is

defined by the grammar:


                ϕ, ψ ::= ⊥ | ι | ¬ϕ | (ϕ ∧ ψ) | Xϕ | (ϕ U ψ),    ι ∈ PROP


[25], [26].

    In this chapter, ϕ, ψ are used to denote grammar non-terminals as well as metavari-

ables ranging over formulae in the language L. The intended meaning is context

dependent.

    The constant ⊥ is the constant for the formula of falsehood. The operator X is

the next-time operator. The formula Xϕ is the assertion that ϕ is true at the next

position of the trace. The operator U is the until operator. The formula (ϕ U ψ) is

the assertion that ψ is true at some position of the trace, and ϕ is true at all positions

before that [25], [26].




                                             26
     Other propositional operators are also introduced as abbreviations, such as


                    (ϕ ∨ ψ) := ¬((¬ϕ) ∧ (¬ψ)),      (ϕ → ψ) := (¬ϕ) ∨ ψ


[22], [23], [24].

     Further, additional temporal operators are also introduced by abbreviation, such

as


                                    F ϕ := (> U ϕ),

                                    Gϕ := ¬F ¬ϕ,

                                (ϕ R ψ) := ¬((¬ϕ) U (¬ψ)),

                                 ϕ W ψ := (ϕ U ψ) ∨ Gϕ,


where > := ¬⊥ [25], [26].

     In the context of the MUDA model, many properties can be expressed as quantified

statements over finite sets in a state x. In this chapter, each such quantified condition

is evaluated in state x, and its truth value is assigned to one of the designated atomic

propositions. For instance, the expression


                              ∀b ∈ B. allocB(b, M (x)) ≤ q(b)


is evaluated in state x. In case the expression is true in state x, then [[x ]] assigns true

to the appropriate atom; otherwise, it assigns false to the appropriate atom [25], [26].

4.1.2     Semantics

    A valuation is defined as a function v : PROP → {true, false} [25], [26]. It is

intuitive that the valuation of the atomic proposition ι under a valuation v is the

truth value of the atomic proposition at a given time step.

    Given a MUDA state x, the valuation [[x ]] is defined such that the truth values

of all atomic propositions are determined by evaluating the designated operational

predicates at state x.

    A trace is defined as an infinite sequence of valuations, given by the function

ρ = (v0 , v1 , v2 , . . .), where the values are indexed by N [25], [26]. The valuation vi

represents the values of the atomic propositions that are true at the given time step i.

    Given a trace ρ, an index i ∈ N, and a formula ϕ, the satisfaction relation is

defined as follows [25], [26]:


              ρ, i |= ι ⇐⇒ vi (ι) = true,

            ρ, i |= ⊥ ⇐⇒ false,

           ρ, i |= ¬ϕ ⇐⇒ ¬(ρ, i |= ϕ),

      ρ, i |= (ϕ ∧ ψ) ⇐⇒ (ρ, i |= ϕ) ∧ (ρ, i |= ψ),

          ρ, i |= Xϕ ⇐⇒ ρ, i + 1 |= ϕ,
                                                                                
      ρ, i |= (ϕ U ψ) ⇐⇒ ∃k ≥ i. ρ, k |= ψ ∧ ∀j. (i ≤ j < k =⇒ ρ, j |= ϕ) .


    The until statement has two parts: the statement ψ must be true sometime in the

future, and the statement ϕ must be true continuously from the current time until

sometime before the future time [25], [26].

     A formula ϕ is valid if and only if |= ϕ, which means for all traces ρ, ρ, 0 |= ϕ [25],

[26].

Lemma 1 (Semantics of F and G). For all traces ρ, all indices i, and all formulae ϕ,


                             ρ, i |= F ϕ ⇐⇒ ∃j ≥ i. ρ, j |= ϕ,

                             ρ, i |= Gϕ ⇐⇒ ∀j ≥ i. ρ, j |= ϕ.


Proof. By definition, F ϕ = (> U ϕ) and Gϕ = ¬F ¬ϕ. Thus, the result follows from

Theorem 2 (Soundness). If ` ϕ, then |= ϕ [25], [26]. This theorem ensures that

4.1.6     Core lemmas for verification

    The proofs of the fairness properties in Sections 4.3 and 4.4 follow similar structures.

First, we show that a MUDA property holds for all states or for a specific state in

Lemma 3. Then we show that we can lift this state-level property to obtain an LTL

property for the valuation trace ρ in Lemma 4.

Lemma 3 (State-G lifting). Let P be a state-level predicate with designated atomic

index ιP . Assume that P holds for all states σ(i) for all i ≥ 0. Then ρ, 0 |= G ιP .

Proof. We assume that P holds for all states σ(i) for all i ≥ 0. Then by definition of

the valuation map, we have ρ(i)(ιP ) = true for all i ≥ 0. Then we have ρ, 0 |= G ιP by

semantic definition of G [25], [26].

Lemma 4 (Witness-F lifting). Let P be a state-level predicate with designated atomic

index ιP . Assume that there exists k ≥ 0 for which P holds for σ(k). Then ρ, 0 |= F ιP .



                                            32
Proof. Suppose that P (σ(k)) for some k ≥ 0. By the valuation map, this implies that

ρ(k)(ιP ) = true. Thus, ρ, k |= ιP . Since ιP is true at index k, we have that ρ, 0 |= F ιP

[25], [26].

Lemma 5 (Stuttering invariance). Let ϕ be an LTL formula that does not have the

X operator. Suppose that two traces are equal except at some index where they stutter.

Then ϕ has the same truth value at that index in both traces.

Proof. The proof is by structural induction on ϕ. For atomic propositions and

propositional connectives, the result follows immediately. For the temporal operators

other than X, the truth conditions for ϕ depend only on whether a subformula holds

at some index within a range, or at all indices within a range. A stutter adds a finite

number of repetitions of a valuation at a single index and does not change the truth

values at any other index. Since ϕ does not have the X operator, it cannot distinguish

whether a fixed number of steps have been taken. Thus, stuttering does not affect

satisfaction. A standard formulation of this result can be found in the literature on

model-checking [25], [26].

4.3     Formalization of Fairness Properties

    For each fairness property, we have a two-step formulation [20].

Step 1. First, we write the fairness property as a predicate on a single MUDA state

      x. This step is operational. It talks about what is true in a given state x. For

                                             35
     example, it talks about whether a chosen trade has priority or whether a price

     is within a certain range.

Step 2. Next, we write the same property as an LTL property on a valuation trace ρ.

     This step talks about time. It talks about whether something is true at every

     step or whether something is true at some step.

This formulation has the advantage that the proofs are straightforward. The opera-

tional step talks about states. The LTL step talks about time using the LTL operators

G and F [25], [26].


  1. Priority fairness.

     In the priority fairness property, priority is relevant only when the protocol is

     matching. During the matching phase, a trade is added to the match record.

     During that step, the protocol must choose the right buyer and the right seller

     based on priority. Outside the matching phase, no trade is chosen. In that case,

     nothing needs to be said.

     To write the priority fairness property as a predicate on a single MUDA state

     x, define prio_step(x) as follows. If phase(x) = P3 and a trade is appended to

     M (x), then the selection satisfies Definitions 3, 4, and 6 [20]. If phase(x) 6= P3 ,

    In this chapter, the executions of the MUDA protocol are presented. An execution

starts with some state, applies the deterministic function δ iteratively, and terminates

when the protocol reaches Phase P7 [20]. Since the LTL formulas are defined over

infinite sequences, the finite execution is extended to an infinite sequence by repeating

models the satisfaction of the corresponding LTL formula.

     In Phase P2 , the protocol generates the priority orders used in matching.

     For buyers, the agent with a higher bid price has a higher priority. In case of a

5.1.3     Phase P3 : Greedy Matching

    In Phase P3 , the protocol repeatedly applies the greedy matching rule [20]. In

each round, the protocol proceeds as follows:

    First, it checks the feasibility of a buyer and a seller. A buyer and a seller are

feasible if the unit price of the buyer is greater than or equal to the unit price of the

seller, and both buyers and sellers have positive residual unit quantity [20].

    Next, it uses a priority rule. The algorithm selects the buyer with the highest

priority among those buyers with at least one feasible seller. Then it selects the

seller with the highest priority among those sellers feasible with the buyer selected in

the previous step. Then, the algorithm trades the maximum unit quantity possible

between the buyer and seller. The unit quantity traded is given by


                           q = min(residB(b, x), residS(s, x))


[20]. This ensures that the quantity traded is always less than or equal to the unit

quantity of both the buyer and seller.

    The match record is then extended by one triple, and the residual unit quantity

of both the buyer and seller is reduced by the unit quantity traded. The algorithm

                                           46
then repeats the previous steps.

                        Maximality           3, 6, 7, 8         4
                        Justified Rejection    2, 7, 8          3
                        Price Fairness      1, 3, 4, 5, 6       5


    The above discussion illustrates how each of the fairness properties of the MUDA

protocol is achieved in the simulation scenarios.


(1) Price Fairness. The scenarios in cases 1, 3, 4, 5, and 6 all involve the compu-

tation of a clearing price p? . This clearing price is calculated according to the rule

specified in Definition 9 [10], [20]. For all these scenarios, the clearing price will always

lie between the marginal seller’s ask price and the marginal buyer’s bid price. In all

these scenarios, the price bounds property, the phase property, and the price selection

property all hold [20], [25], [26].

                                             62
(2) Priority Fairness. In case 4, the strict lexicographic priority rule is illustrated

[20], [21]. When the buyer bid prices are the same, then the execution is governed

by the timestamp. In the execution trace, the buyer and seller priority relations are

maintained as specified in Chapter 3. In the execution trace, the priority fairness

property is maintained at each matching step [20], [25], [26].


(3) Quantity Fairness. The executions of cases 3, 5, and 6 show the quantity

fairness property [20], [25], [26]. For these cases, it is ensured that the quantities

allocated do not exceed the quantities specified, and the remaining quantities are

nonnegative [20]. For case 5, it is ensured that the over-stated demand does not result

in the allocation exceeding the supply of the seller [20]. For case 6, it is ensured that

the under-stated demand results in the allocation being reduced accordingly [20].


(4) Match Finality. In Case 1, we verify if the match is appended to the cumulative

match record and maintained throughout all subsequent states of Phases P4 , P5 , P6 ,

and P7 [20]. The match (b1 , s1 ) is maintained even if a higher bid is made by a

subsequent buyer [20]. This illustrates the monotonicity of the match record [20], [25],

[26].


(5) Maximality. The verification of Cases 3, 6, and 7 checks whether the matching

terminates when no feasible pair is left in the terminal state. All feasible trades have

been made before entering Phase P4 [20]. In Case 8, we have a degenerate market

with no sellers in the initial state [20]. As no feasible pair is available, we immediately

transition to Phase P4 and the final match record is empty [20].



                                            63
(6) Justified Rejection. In Cases 2, 7, and 8, the rejected buyers at termination

do not have a feasible pair [20]. In Case 2, the buyer b1 is rejected and does not have

a feasible pair with any seller [20]. In Case 7, the rejected buyers have a residual

demand and do not have a feasible ask [20]. In Case 8, all buyers are rejected since the

seller set is empty [20]. The execution traces verify the justified rejection requirement

[20], [25], [26].


(7) Integration of Formal and Execution Level Evidence. The execution traces

satisfy the fairness theorem for the respective case [25], [26]. The operational semantics,

the temporal interpretation, and the proof system mutually verify one another [20],

[25], [26]. The execution scenarios verify the formal model on the execution level. The

correctness of the model follows as a result of the formal verification in Chapter 4 [25],

[26], [27].

Chapter 7

Conclusion

    This thesis has formally verified the fairness properties of the MUDA protocol with

respect to its seven deterministic phases [20]. The MUDA protocol has been formally

modeled as a deterministic STS and analyzed using LTL for formal verification [25],

[26]. The MUDA protocol is a batch, single-round auction protocol [20]. All matching,

pricing, update of residual quantities, and rejection occur during a single execution

[20]. Therefore, fairness is verified at the execution level and not at the matching level.

    The case study and simulation scenarios have been used to verify the fairness

properties of the MUDA protocol [20]. The formal verification has shown that the

MUDA protocol satisfies Priority Fairness, Quantity Fairness, Price Fairness, Match

Finality, Maximality, and Justified Rejection for all execution traces generated by the

model [25], [26]. The fairness properties of the MUDA protocol have been formally

expressed using the LTL operators G, F , and U , which relate state-level properties to

execution-level properties [25], [26].

    The formal verification of the MUDA protocol has followed a layered structure


                                            65
with respect to the phases of the MUDA protocol [20]. The operational semantics of

the STS model of the MUDA protocol have defined the components of the MUDA

protocol, namely feasibility, priority ordering, update of residual quantities, greedy

matching, clearing price determination, and rejection. All these components of the

MUDA protocol have been formally expressed as atomic propositions [25], [26].

    The model also ensures well-defined execution behavior. Termination of the

matching phase is ensured since the total residual quantity is strictly reduced in every

successful trade [20], [21]. After termination of the matching phase, execution of the

remaining phases is deterministic and does not modify the submitted orders, residual

quantities, match record, and clearing price [20]. Since the fairness properties do not

use the next-state operator, there is no effect on the satisfaction of LTL properties

due to stutter extension after termination [25], [26].

    The operational definitions, temporal properties, and simulation results are con-

sistent with the mechanically derived theorems. Every fairness theorem derived in

Chapter 4 corresponds to the execution behavior analyzed in Chapters 5 and 6. Exe-

cution of the STS does not violate any of the fairness properties. All theorems specific

to the MUDA protocol are mechanically verified in the Rocq proof assistant (formerly

Coq) [27]. No lemmas are admitted in the verification process.

    This work shows the ability to verify fairness properties of the MUDA protocol

using LTL over complete executions in the context of a deterministic STS [25], [26].

This methodology can be extended to verify fairness properties under different priority

rules and alternative pricing schemes, including controlled scenarios of nondeterminism,

provided that appropriate operational semantics and temporal properties are defined.
